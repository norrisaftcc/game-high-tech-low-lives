# GM GUIDE: USING KICKERS AND LAYERED IDENTITIES

## WHAT IS A KICKER?

A **kicker** is character background that launches play rather than sitting dormant. It's not backstory—it's active situation. Characters don't have a past that *might* become relevant. They have complications *currently in motion* that demand response.

**Poor backstory**: "My character used to work for a corp."  
**Good kicker**: "My character stole corporate property three months ago and the bounty just increased."

Kickers create immediate pressure. They give players something to worry about, protect, pursue, or escape from session one. They make the first job matter because it intersects with existing complications.

## THE THREE-LAYER STRUCTURE

Our pregenerated characters use three-layer identity structure. This isn't deception mechanics—it's **perspective management**. Each layer is true from someone's viewpoint.

### Layer One: THE SURFACE
**What casual observers see. The performed role.**

This is the character's public presentation. Corporate employee. Influencer. Smuggler. It's not fake—it's functional. People believe this layer because the character sells it convincingly.

**Example (Ann Sho)**:  
"Sable Harker, AlgoCratic employee B2C7, ORANGE clearance. Hired three months ago. Code quality reviewer. Loyalty Score 7.2/10. Productive. Detail-oriented."

**GM Use**: NPCs interact with this layer. Corporate security sees "employee." Other runners see "person with access." This layer provides cover and creates constraints—blow the cover, lose the access.

### Layer Two: WHAT OTHERS KNOW
**The reputation. The street truth.**

This is what people in the know believe. Other criminals, resistance networks, people who see past the surface. This layer might contradict Layer One, but it's still not the full truth.

**Example (Ann Sho)**:  
"Ann Sho, handle shodann. Works inside AlgoCratic-Fujikara. Selling access? Maybe. Selling data? Probably. Another corpo rat gone freelance."

**GM Use**: This is what the crew believes initially. They think they know who they're working with. Let them be right... or wrong. Reveal truth through play, not exposition.

### Layer Three: WHAT YOU KNOW  
**The character's actual truth. Maybe.**

This is what the player knows. The real mission, the real history, the real motivation. Or is it? Even this layer might be performance—the character lying to themselves.

**Example (Ann Sho)**:  
"Samantha Hayes O'Day. DEF CON University. g0 network operative. Teaching students to hack the planet through corporate 'security audits.' Resistance mission wrapped in corporate cover."

**GM Use**: Let players define this layer during character creation. Then question it through play. When choices contradict stated truth, ask: "Is this who you really are, or who you want to be?"

## LAYERED IDENTITIES IN PLAY

### During Investigation/Social Scenes
**Characters code-switch between layers constantly.**

With corporate NPCs: Surface layer (performing the role)  
With crew: Layer two (professional criminal persona)  
Alone/stressed: Layer three bleeds through (actual motivation)

**GM Technique**: Notice which layer the player uses in different contexts. When they slip—using Layer Three language in Layer One situation—that's character development happening.

**Example**:  
Player (as Ann): "I'll review the security code for 'optimization opportunities.'" [Layer One language]  
GM: "The AlgoCratic manager looks pleased. Do you actually run the audit, or...?"  
Player: "I'm finding backdoors. The audit is cover." [Layer Two action]  
GM: "While you work, your digital clone sends you a message: 'Behavioral deviation detected.' How do you feel about that?"  
Player: [Layer Three response reveals actual emotional stakes]

### During Complications
**Force choices that reveal which layer is authentic.**

When pressure builds, people act on instinct. Design complications that make layers conflict:

- Layer One (Surface) says: Maintain corporate cover  
- Layer Two (Street) says: Profit from the situation  
- Layer Three (Truth?) says: Complete the actual mission

**Example Complication**:  
Ann discovers AlgoCratic is about to fire a student she's been teaching to hack through "bug reports." Keeping cover means saying nothing. Street logic says use it as leverage. Her resistance mission says protect the student.

**What she chooses reveals who she actually is.**

### During Betrayal/Trust Moments  
**Layers can be weapons or bridges.**

Revealing a deeper layer to another character is huge. It's vulnerability. It's also potential leverage.

**Example**:  
Ann tells Reyes about the g0 network mission. That's Layer Three information given to someone who knew Layer Two. She's either building trust or setting up betrayal. Both are valid play.

**GM Response**: Make that revelation matter. NPCs who learn deeper layers react. Crew members adjust expectations. Information has weight.

## OPERATIONAL PROTOCOLS AS FLAVOR

Some characters have explicit protocols—challenge/response codes, communication signals, backup plans. These aren't just flavor text. They're **mechanical triggers for plot**.

### Using Protocols to Create Tension

**Ann's g0 Network Protocols**:
- Challenge: "frotz" / Response: "plugh"  
- Coffee timing is steganographic messaging  
- Badge number B2C7 encodes messages

**GM Use**:  
- Drop the challenge phrase in NPC dialogue. See if player recognizes it.  
- Have someone order coffee at the "wrong" time—is it signal or coincidence?  
- Corporate security notices pattern in badge access logs. Are they close to breaking the code?

**Reyes's Naval Background**:
- Knows patrol patterns from inside  
- Uses military protocols when stressed  
- The Mirasol responds to naval command structure

**GM Use**:  
- Patrol boat hails using old military codes. Does he respond instinctively and blow cover?  
- Boat interface requires commands in specific format. When panicked, does he revert to training?  
- Former colleague recognizes his piloting style. How does he handle it?

### Protocols Create Player Investment

When players see their protocols matter mechanically, they engage deeper:
- Recognition codes can be compelled (you gave the response automatically—wrong person heard it)  
- Signals can be misinterpreted (GM declares coffee timing was noticed by surveillance)  
- Backup plans can fail (the emergency exit is blocked—now what?)

## LAUNCHING PLAY WITH KICKERS

### Session Zero: Building Pressure

After character creation, **ask players what their character fears most** about their current situation:

- Ann: "What happens if your clone reports you?"  
- Kaida: "What if your followers find out the streams are edited?"  
- Reyes: "What if the Mirasol breaks down in deep water?"

**Write these down.** These are ticking clocks. Not immediate threats—ongoing pressure.

### Session One: Convergence

The first job isn't random. It **intersects multiple kickers**:

**Bad First Job**: "Steal generic data from generic corp."

**Good First Job**: "Steal data from AlgoCratic division that's investigating Ann's behavioral deviations, using Kaida's corporate sponsorship access, requiring extraction by Reyes through waters his former patrol used to cover."

Each character's kicker becomes relevant immediately. The job threatens their covers, triggers their fears, or offers solution to their problems.

### Mid-Campaign: Escalation

Kickers aren't static. They escalate:

- Ann's clone gets smarter, harder to fool  
- Kaida's sponsors demand exclusivity  
- Reyes's bounty increases after each job  
- Marcus's dual clients discover each other

**Every 3-4 sessions, advance one kicker's clock.** Make the pressure increase. Force characters to adapt.

## BALANCING TRUTH AND PERFORMANCE

### The Wolfe/Vance Approach

Characters in Gene Wolfe novels often have three names, three identities. The reader discovers which is "real" slowly, through contradiction and choice. Jack Vance's characters perform competence with style, but the performance *is* competence—it's real and fake simultaneously.

**Apply this to NPCs and complications**:

**Wolfe technique**: Information is true from one perspective, false from another. Let players piece together reality.

**Vance technique**: Style and substance are the same thing. Characters who perform competence *are* competent. The performance is the reality.

### Questions to Ask During Play

**Don't ask**: "Are you Sable or Ann?"  
**Ask**: "Which one are you being right now, and why?"

**Don't ask**: "Is your motivation real?"  
**Ask**: "When you made that choice, what were you protecting?"

**Don't ask**: "Are you lying?"  
**Ask**: "Do you believe what you just said?"

Let players discover their characters through decisions under pressure. The truth isn't what they wrote during creation—it's what they choose when layers conflict.

## COMMON MISTAKES TO AVOID

### Mistake 1: Treating Surface as Fake
**Wrong**: "Your corporate cover is just a disguise."  
**Right**: "Your corporate role is real work with real consequences. You're just doing it for different reasons."

Surface layer isn't mask—it's functional identity that has to convince because it's tested daily.

### Mistake 2: Revealing Layers Too Fast
**Wrong**: Session two, NPCs already know everyone's secrets.  
**Right**: Let layers peel gradually through player choice, not GM exposition.

Discovery should feel earned. If Ann's resistance mission gets exposed, it should be because she chose to reveal it or failed to protect it—not because GM decided it was time.

### Mistake 3: Ignoring Protocol Details  
**Wrong**: "Yeah, your signal codes are neat flavor."  
**Right**: "Security noticed your coffee pattern. They're watching now."

If you gave players protocols, compel them. Make them matter mechanically and narratively.

### Mistake 4: Forcing One True Identity
**Wrong**: "Okay, which persona is the REAL you?"  
**Right**: "You've been performing so long, does the distinction still matter?"

Some characters genuinely don't know which layer is authentic. That's feature, not bug. Let the ambiguity persist.

## EXAMPLE: FIRST SESSION OPENING

**Setup**: Marcus calls crew for job. He needs data extracted from AlgoCratic facility. Simple retrieval. Good pay.

**Kicker Intersection**:

**Ann's Kicker Active**: This is her workplace. She has access. But her clone has been flagging deviations. Using her access for criminal work is maximum risk.

**Kaida's Kicker Active**: Apex Chrome sponsors AlgoCratic's security systems. If she's caught infiltrating a client facility, she loses the sponsorship.

**Reyes's Kicker Active**: Extraction requires navigating waters patrolled by Fujikara-Pacific. His former unit. If they ID the Mirasol, arrest is automatic.

**First Scene**: Marcus presents the job. Players realize immediately this isn't simple—it cuts through their covers. Do they take it anyway? What do they negotiate?

**Second Scene**: Planning. Each character must decide which layer to use. Ann could:
- Use corporate access (Layer One - risky to cover)  
- Hire external hacker (Layer Two - safer but expensive)  
- Contact g0 network for support (Layer Three - reveals mission)

**Third Scene**: Execution. Something goes wrong (of course). Complications force choices that reveal which layer is authentic. When crisis hits, who are they really?

**Debrief**: Marcus pays them. Asks no questions about methods. But now each character's kicker has escalated slightly:
- Ann's clone logged anomalous access  
- Kaida's producer Lian asks pointed questions  
- Reyes's patrol boat got spotted on security footage

The job succeeded. The kickers advanced. Next session's pressure is higher.

## SUMMARY: KICKERS AS ENGINE

Kickers aren't backstory. They're **ongoing situations that demand response**.

Layered identities aren't **deception mechanics**. They're **perspective management** that creates dramatic tension.

Protocols aren't **flavor text**. They're **mechanical triggers** for complications.

**Good kicker formula**:
[Character] has [situation currently in motion] that will [escalate without action]. They [chose this/were forced into this] because [motivation]. The [consequence] is [approaching].

**Use kickers to**:
- Design jobs that matter personally  
- Create complications that force revealing choices  
- Escalate pressure gradually across campaign  
- Let players discover their characters through action

**The goal**: Characters who are charged with immediate complications, layered identities that create dramatic tension, and ongoing pressure that makes every job personal.

Players won't say "what's my motivation?" They'll say "how do I survive this week?"

That's when the game gets good.

---

*"You don't play these characters by deciding who they are. You play them by choosing what they do when their layers contradict. Truth is what survives when the masks crack."*

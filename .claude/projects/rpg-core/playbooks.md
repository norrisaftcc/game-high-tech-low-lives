# Playbook Structure & Format

[← Back to RPG Core](./README.md) | [← Back to Hub](../../README.md)

## Overview

This document defines the complete structure and format for High Tech, Low Lives playbooks. All playbooks follow this consistent template to ensure players can easily learn and reference their character options.

---

## Playbook Template Structure

Every playbook uses this 15-section format:

### 1. Identity & Concept
**What you are, not what you do**

Opens with evocative paragraph establishing:
- Core identity/role in world
- Primary tension or duality
- How you relate to corporate/street divide
- What makes this playbook distinct

Example (Infiltrator):
> You're the ghost in the corporate machine, three faces for three worlds. Surface identity clears security checkpoints. Street alias moves through resistance networks. Your real name? Maybe you've forgotten which one that is. You find vulnerabilities—in systems, in people, in the gaps between what they say and what they mean.

### 2. Name Suggestions
**4-6 options per identity layer**

Lists evocative names that reinforce character concept:
- Corporate/surface names (professional, bland)
- Street/alias names (chosen, meaningful)
- [Optional] Real names (authentic, personal)

Format:
```markdown
**Surface**: Chen, Rodriguez, Kim, O'Brien
**Street**: Cipher, Ghost, Null, Sable
```

### 3. Look Options
**Visual descriptors, not mechanics**

3-4 categories with 4 options each:
- **Build**: Compact, Athletic, Soft, Weathered
- **Face**: Sharp, Warm, Forgettable, Striking
- **Eyes**: Cold, Tired, Alert, Kind
- **Presentation**: Corporate Clean, Street Casual, Technical Functional, Performance Ready

Choose one from each category to define appearance.

### 4. Stats (Pre-Assigned)
**No point-buy, playbook defines you**

Each playbook has fixed stat array:
- One stat at +3 (primary)
- Optional second stat at +2
- Others distributed +0 to +2
- Total stat bonus: +6 to +8

Display format:
```markdown
BODY:     +0
REFLEXES: +1
CODE:     +3  (primary)
TECH:     +2
COOL:     +2
```

Stats are **pre-assigned** to reinforce playbook identity. No customization.

### 5. Starting Stress Tracks
**Base 3 boxes each, modified by chrome**

Display format:
```markdown
MEAT:    [ ] [ ] [ ]
NERVES:  [ ] [ ] [ ]
SYSTEMS: [ ] [ ] [ ]
```

Chrome selections will modify these (typically +Systems, -Meat or -Nerves).

### 6. Chrome Options
**Choose 2-3 from list, shows stress trade-offs**

Each chrome entry includes:
- **Name** (evocative, specific)
- **Description** (brief, functional, establishes fiction)
- **Stress modification** (always show trade-off)
- **Mechanical benefit or trigger** (optional)

Format:
```markdown
**Neural Deck Port**
Instant corporate access, zero-delay code intrusion. The port sits at the base of your skull—smooth titanium with a data interface that never quite stops itching.
**Systems** +1 box / **Meat** -1 box
When you interface with corporate systems, you're already inside before security notices the login.

**Subvocal Communicator**
Encrypted comms that look like you're thinking to yourself. Thread-thin implant along your throat, sub-audio transmission.
**Systems** +1 box / **Nerves** -1 box
When you need to coordinate silently, you can communicate with anyone on your network without moving your lips.
```

**Chrome Design Principles**:
- Always show fictional positioning (what it looks/feels like)
- Always show mechanical cost (stress trade-off)
- Fiction suggests application, mechanics support it
- Cost implies maintenance/vulnerability

### 7. Talents (Choose 2)
**Playbook-specific abilities and moves**

5-7 options to choose from. Each talent:
- Has clear fictional trigger
- Provides mechanical benefit
- Reinforces playbook identity
- Shows how to be awesome at your thing

Format:
```markdown
**I Know This System**
When you analyze security (physical or digital) in a corporate environment, roll +Code.
**10+**: Choose 3
**7-9**: Choose 1
- Identify the primary vulnerability
- Spot the monitoring blind spots
- Determine who designed it (and their patterns)
- Assess time needed to exploit it

**Multiple Faces**
You maintain 2-3 distinct identities with separate documentation, contacts, and histories. When you switch identities, the world treats you as that person—until you give them reason not to.
```

**Talent Design Principles**:
- Start with fictional trigger ("When you...")
- Specify stat to roll (usually primary or secondary)
- Define clear outcomes (10+/7-9/6-)
- Or provide fictional permission (no roll, always true)

### 8. Aspects
**High Concept, Trouble, Connection**

Three aspects that drive story:

**High Concept**: Who you are, what you do
- Should capture playbook identity + personal twist
- Examples: "Three Faces for Three Worlds", "3.2M Followers Watch Me Work"

**Trouble**: What complicates your life
- Built-in problem, constant pressure
- Examples: "Which Face Is Real Anymore?", "Authenticity Is Performance"

**Connection**: Someone who matters
- NPC with wants, needs, complications
- Examples: "Marcus Kain Recruited Me, Now He's Paranoid", "Manager Chen Is Testing Me"

Format with prompts:
```markdown
**High Concept**: _____________________
Who you are and what defines you. This is your identity.

**Trouble**: _____________________
What complicates your life, creates pressure, demands choices.

**Connection**: _____________________
Someone who matters to you. They'll show up, ask for things, need you.
```

### 9. Starting Gear
**What you have, not what you shop for**

Simple list of equipment that:
- Reinforces identity
- Provides fictional tools
- Suggests how you operate
- Doesn't require mechanical tracking

Format:
```markdown
**GEAR**
- Corporate credentials (surface identity, valid access)
- Encrypted commlink (disposable, burn after 48 hours)
- Multi-tool (technical specs exceed consumer grade)
- Secure storage (dead drop locations, encrypted drives)
- Second set of clothes (for the other you)
```

**Gear Design Principles**:
- Evocative names, brief descriptions
- Suggests capability without mechanics
- Reinforces playbook theme
- Implies maintenance/vulnerability

### 10. Example Consequences
**One per stress track, teaches by showing**

Shows what consequences look like for this playbook:

```markdown
**CONSEQUENCES**

**Meat** (Mild): Dislocated finger - chrome hand loose in the socket
**Nerves** (Moderate): Identity bleed - answering to the wrong name
**Systems** (Severe): Neural deck corruption - corporate security has your access signature
```

**Consequence Design Principles**:
- Specific to playbook, not generic
- Shows severity through description
- Suggests fictional complications
- Teaches through example, not rule explanation

### 11. How to Play
**Good at, bad at, when to roll**

Three-part guide:

**You're good at**:
- 3-4 things this playbook excels at
- Fictional positioning, not mechanics
- Shows when you're awesome

**You're bad at**:
- 2-3 things this playbook struggles with
- Creates vulnerability, drives story
- Shows when you need help

**Roll your primary stat when**:
- 3-4 specific fictional triggers
- Clear examples of play
- Teaches when to engage mechanics

Format:
```markdown
**You're good at**: Finding vulnerabilities, switching identities, reading social patterns, accessing restricted spaces, gathering intelligence without being noticed.

**You're bad at**: Direct confrontation, trusting people, maintaining real relationships, staying in one identity too long, handling physical violence.

**Roll +Code when**: Analyzing security systems, spoofing credentials, identifying patterns in data, exploiting social engineering vectors, determining who designed a system.
```

### 12. Signature Move
**One unique playbook-specific move**

The move that defines this playbook:
- Clear fictional trigger
- Uses primary or secondary stat
- Provides interesting choices
- Reinforces identity and tension

Format:
```markdown
**[MOVE NAME]**
When you [specific trigger], roll +[Stat].

**On 10+**: [Strong success, you get what you want]

**On 7-9**: [Success with cost, complication, or hard choice]

**On 6-**: [Failure with consequence, GM makes a move]
```

**Move Design Principles**:
- Trigger should be specific, not broad
- Outcomes should create interesting choices
- 7-9 should complicate, not just reduce success
- Move should feel like this playbook's core competence

### 13. Relationships (Phase Four)
**Connection to other PCs**

Integration with other characters:
- What you did together
- What you owe/are owed
- Shared history
- Ongoing tension

Format shows prompts for each other PC:
```markdown
**PHASE FOUR: CONNECTIONS**

For each other character, establish one:

**[Character Name]**:
- You trust them with ________________
- They know you as ________________ (which identity?)
- You owe them for ________________
- They don't know about ________________
```

### 14. Advancement
**What you get when you grow**

Options for character development:
- New talent from your playbook
- +1 to any stat (max +3)
- New chrome (with stress trade-off)
- Clear severe consequence
- New aspect
- Move from another playbook
- Create new identity/alias
- Train stat to +4 (requires multiple advances)

Format:
```markdown
**ADVANCEMENT**

When you earn an advance, choose one:
- [ ] New talent from your list
- [ ] +1 to any stat (max +3)
- [ ] Additional chrome (accept stress trade-off)
- [ ] Clear a severe consequence permanently
- [ ] Establish a new aspect
- [ ] Take a talent from another playbook
- [ ] Create a new identity with full documentation
- [ ] Train your primary stat to +4 (requires 2 advances)
```

### 15. Discovery Questions
**Answer through play, not creation**

5-8 questions that:
- Reveal character through play
- Create story hooks
- Establish setting details
- Drive investigation of identity

Format:
```markdown
**DISCOVERY QUESTIONS**
Answer these through play, not at character creation:

- Which identity feels most real to you now?
- What did you sacrifice to become what you are?
- Who taught you how to disappear?
- What does your digital clone know that you don't?
- When did you last tell someone your real name?
- What are you really looking for in these systems?
- Who's watching you that you haven't noticed yet?
- What will you do when the chrome fails mid-job?
```

## Playbook-Specific Considerations

### The Infiltrator
- **Primary tension**: Identity fragmentation (which face is real?)
- **Chrome focus**: Neural interfaces, social engineering tech
- **Signature capability**: System analysis and vulnerability exploitation
- **Weakness**: Emotional connection, trusting people

### The Influencer
- **Primary tension**: Performance vs authenticity
- **Chrome focus**: Broadcasting tech, visual enhancement
- **Signature capability**: Turning attention into advantage
- **Weakness**: Privacy, operating quietly, genuine moments

### The Nomad
- **Primary tension**: Freedom vs maintenance costs
- **Chrome focus**: Environmental adaptation, boat integration
- **Signature capability**: Navigation, water world expertise
- **Weakness**: Corporate spaces, staying in one place

## Writing Style for Playbooks

### Mechanics Text
**Terse, active, functional** (Orwell's rules)
- Short words, active voice, cut unnecessary words
- "Choose two chrome" not "You may wish to select two chrome options"
- "Roll +Code" not "The player rolls 4dF and adds their Code modifier"

### Flavor Text
**Evocative, specific, cinematic**
- Show the world through this character's eyes
- Specific details over generic descriptions
- "Your boat knows these waters better than you do" not "You are a skilled pilot"

### Examples
**Show rules in action, don't explain**
- Consequences demonstrate severity through fiction
- How to Play shows triggers through specific scenes
- Discovery Questions reveal character through prompts

## Format Consistency

### Headers
- Use sentence case, not Title Case
- Exception: Game terms (Chrome, Fate Points, Aspect)
- H2 for major sections, H3 for subsections

### Lists
- Bulleted for options, examples, non-sequential
- Numbered for procedures, steps, sequences
- Checkboxes for advancement options

### Emphasis
- **Bold** for game terms, section headers, chrome names
- *Italic* for thoughts, inner voice, emphasis
- `Backticks` for stats, dice notation, inline mechanics

### Spacing
- Blank line before/after headers
- Blank line between list items when items are multi-line
- Three dashes (`---`) for section breaks

## Quality Checklist

Before finalizing any playbook, verify:

- [ ] All 15 sections present and complete
- [ ] Stats total +6 to +8 (balanced with other playbooks)
- [ ] Chrome shows stress trade-offs clearly
- [ ] Talents have clear triggers and outcomes
- [ ] Aspects reinforce playbook identity and tension
- [ ] Gear reinforces theme without mechanical overhead
- [ ] Consequences are specific to playbook, not generic
- [ ] How to Play section teaches through examples
- [ ] Signature move uses primary/secondary stat
- [ ] Discovery questions drive story, not just background
- [ ] Tone matches: terse for mechanics, evocative for flavor
- [ ] Format is consistent with other playbooks
- [ ] Examples show, explanations are minimal
- [ ] Print-friendly (2-4 pages)

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Don't**:
- Explain mechanics that format already teaches
- Use generic cyberpunk clichés without subversion
- Create chrome without stress trade-offs
- Write talents that are always active (need clear triggers)
- Make aspects that don't create tension
- List gear that requires mechanical tracking
- Write consequences that could apply to any playbook
- Front-load rules explanation (show through structure)
- Create stat arrays that don't reinforce identity
- Skip Discovery Questions (they drive play)

**Do**:
- Trust format to teach the rules
- Use specific, setting-appropriate details
- Show chrome costs through stress modifications
- Give talents clear fictional triggers
- Make aspects that complicate the character's life
- Keep gear evocative and low-maintenance
- Make consequences reveal character vulnerabilities
- Let examples demonstrate rules
- Ensure stats support playbook's core competence
- Use Discovery Questions to explore identity

## The Curtain Drawn Back: Understanding Shared Imagined Space

Picture this: Five people sit at a table. No board, no miniatures, just dice and conversation. Yet they're all seeing the same rain-slick street in the neon-soaked sprawl, hearing the same corporate enforcer's boots splash through puddles, feeling the same tension as the netrunner's fingers hover over their deck.

This is the **shared imagined space**—the collective fiction that exists between all players. It's not in any one person's head. It emerges from conversation, from proposal and acceptance, from the constant negotiation of "what's true now?"

The game isn't the dice. The game isn't the rules. The game is what everyone agrees is happening in that fictional space. Everything else—mechanics, dice, character sheets—these are just tools to help decide what changes in that space.

## The Core Loop: Suggest, Negotiate, Resolve

Play follows a simple pattern:

1. **Someone suggests a change to the fiction**
   - "I jack into the corporate mainframe"
   - "The enforcer kicks down the door"
   - "Rain starts falling, heavy enough to obscure thermal signatures"

2. **The table determines if this requires negotiation**
   - Is it contested? ("The ICE tries to fry your brain")
   - Is it uncertain? ("Can you reach the roof before they spot you?")
   - Is it dramatically interesting? ("This could go either way...")

3. **Resolution enters the fiction**
   - Dice determine outcome when needed
   - Agreement determines outcome when not
   - The fiction changes permanently

## Say Yes or Roll the Dice

The fundamental principle: **Default to "yes."**

When a player says their character does something:
- If no one objects and it's within their capability: **It happens.**
- If someone has a conflicting interest: **Roll dice.**
- If failure would be interesting: **Roll dice.**
- If success is uncertain: **Roll dice.**

But never roll dice just to roll dice. Every roll must answer the question: "Can I change the fiction in this specific way?"

Examples:
- "I buy a coffee from the street vendor" → **Yes**, it happens
- "I shoot the enforcer" → **Roll dice**, they oppose
- "I know a guy who can get us in" → **Roll dice** if it matters, **Yes** if it's color
- "I decode the encrypted file" → **Roll dice**, failure has consequences

## Character Aspects: Who You Are in the Fiction

Every character has three core aspects that position them in the world:

### High Concept
Your core identity. What would someone know about you from across the room?
- "Ex-Corporate Netrunner Gone Rogue"
- "Street Doc with Military-Grade Chrome"
- "Anarchist Courier Who Never Breaks a Deal"

### Trouble
What makes your life complicated? What pulls you into dangerous situations?
- "The Yakuza Owns My Debt"
- "Can't Walk Away from a Conspiracy"
- "My Chrome is Slowly Killing Me"

### Connection
One relationship, place, or thing that grounds you in the world.
- "My Sister Still Works for Nexus Corp"
- "The Rust Quarter Knows My Name"
- "This Encrypted Memory Core Could Change Everything"

These aspects are **true in the fiction**. When you invoke them (spending a Fate point for +2 or a reroll), you're saying "because this is true about me, I should succeed here." When they're compelled against you (gaining a Fate point for complications), the fiction is saying "because this is true about you, your life gets complicated."

## Character Positioning: Build Points

You have **8 Build Points** to spend customizing your character beyond basic competency. These represent training, augmentation, or inherent advantages.

### Talents (Variable Cost)

**Extra Stress Track** (3 points)
Add one additional stress track with 3 boxes. Choose its nature:
- **Chrome**: Cybernetic redundancy and backup systems
- **Rep**: Social capital and favors you can burn
- **Resources**: Gear, money, and material assets

**Specialized Training** (2 points each)
+2 to a specific stat in a narrow circumstance:
- Body when fighting in zero-G
- Reflexes when piloting ground vehicles
- Code when dealing with military ICE
- Tech when working with bioware
- Cool when facing corporate intimidation

**Signature Gear** (1 point)
One piece of equipment that's exceptional:
- A weapon that ignores one level of armor
- Chrome that can't be detected by scanners
- A vehicle that's faster than it should be
- Software that does one thing perfectly

**Connected** (2 points)
You have an established relationship with a faction:
- Corporate insider who's not burned yet
- Gang member in good standing
- Black market dealer's favorite customer
- Government contact who owes you

**Natural** (4 points)
You have no cybernetic augmentation. Zero chrome. This is increasingly rare and valuable:
- Immune to EMP and electronic warfare
- Can pass through high-security biometric scanners
- Certain groups trust you inherently
- Your Meat stress track has 5 boxes instead of 3

### Example Builds

**The Corporate Samurai** (8 points)
- Extra Stress Track: Chrome (3 points)
- Specialized Training: Body when using melee weapons (2 points)
- Specialized Training: Reflexes when in combat (2 points)
- Signature Gear: Monomolecular blade (1 point)

**The Ghost** (8 points)
- Natural (4 points)
- Specialized Training: Reflexes when avoiding detection (2 points)
- Connected: Underground railroad for extracting corporate defectors (2 points)

**The Fixer** (8 points)
- Extra Stress Track: Rep (3 points)
- Extra Stress Track: Resources (3 points)
- Connected: Criminal underworld (2 points)

## Stakes and Consequences

When you roll dice, everyone must agree on the stakes **before** rolling:
- **What happens if you succeed?**
- **What happens if you fail?**
- **What happens on a tie?**

These stakes are **binding**. When the dice settle, the agreed-upon outcome enters the fiction immediately. No negotiation after the fact. No "yes, but..." added after seeing the result.

### Single Roll Stakes Examples

**"I'm hacking the corporate server to steal the prototype specs"**
- Success: You have the specs, clean, untraced
- Failure: You trigger an alarm and they trace your location
- Tie: You get the specs but they're encrypted with military-grade ICE

**"I'm talking my way past the security checkpoint"**
- Success: They wave you through, no questions asked
- Failure: They detain you for questioning
- Tie: They let you pass but mark you for surveillance

### Extended Contest Stakes

Sometimes the stakes are too big for one roll. Set a victory condition and track progress:

**"We're racing through the sprawl to reach the extraction point"**
- Victory: First to 3 successes
- Your success: You gain ground, lose pursuit, or clear an obstacle
- Their success: They close in, corner you, or block your route
- Final stakes: Whoever wins determines if you escape or get caught

## The Table's Authority

Different participants have different authority over the fiction:

**Players** have authority over:
- Their character's actions and intentions
- Their character's internal world
- Proposing aspects about the world connected to their character

**The GM** has authority over:
- NPCs' actions and intentions
- The world's response to player actions
- Environmental details and complications
- Pacing and scene framing

**Everyone** shares authority over:
- Whether proposed fiction makes sense
- Whether stakes are fair and interesting
- Whether aspects can be invoked or compelled
- The tone and direction of the story

## Quick Reference: Core Mechanics

**Stats**: Body, Reflexes, Code, Tech, Cool (+0 to +3)

**Roll**: 4 Fate dice + Stat vs. Difficulty or Opposition

**Difficulties**:
- +0 Routine
- +2 Challenging
- +4 Expert
- +6 Near-impossible
- +8 Miraculous

**Stress Tracks** (3 boxes base):
- Meat (physical harm)
- Nerves (psychological strain)
- Systems (technical damage)

**Consequences** (persist until addressed):
- Minor (2 stress): Heals with a scene's downtime
- Moderate (4 stress): Heals with deliberate effort
- Severe (6 stress): Heals with significant time and cost

**Fate Points**: Start with 3
- Spend to invoke aspects (+2 or reroll)
- Gain when aspects complicate your life
- Refresh to 3 at session start

## Playing the Game

The game lives in the conversation. Every rule, every mechanic, every die roll exists to serve one purpose: helping everyone at the table agree on what happens next in your shared fiction.

Trust the process:
1. Describe what you want
2. Negotiate if needed
3. Roll if uncertain
4. Accept the outcome
5. Build on what's new

The best moments come from unexpected outcomes. When the dice say something surprising happens, embrace it. That's not the game failing—that's the game working exactly as intended.

Your character is not you. They're a vehicle for exploring this fiction. When they fail, when they suffer, when their Trouble makes their life hell—that's not you losing. That's you playing.

Say yes or roll the dice. Change the fiction. Play to find out what happens.

*Welcome to the shared imagined space. Let's see what we build together.*

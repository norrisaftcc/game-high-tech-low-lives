## THE CORE DESIGN INSIGHT

### Traditional RPG Question:
*"What can your character do?"*

### This Game's Question:
*"What can YOU AS A PLAYER declare about the fiction?"*

**Example**:
- **Traditional**: "I want to hack the door. Do I roll Code?"
- **This Game**: "I use Backdoor Specialist. I audited this facility before—I'm automatically in. GM, what complication do I find inside?"

The player with the Infiltrator playbook doesn't just have +3 Code. They have **narrative authority** to say "I've been here before" and make it retroactively true.

This is collaborative storytelling with mechanical support.

## THE THREE TYPES OF MECHANICS

Every mechanic in this game grants one of three types of agency:

### 1. FICTIONAL POSITIONING
**What it grants**: Established facts about what you can attempt

**Examples**:
- Infiltrator's Corporate Credentials: "I can attempt to pass as corporate employee"
- Hacker's Neural Interface: "I can attempt to jack in directly"
- Nomad's Boat: "I can attempt to transport people"

**Player authority**: Can propose actions within this space  
**GM authority**: Determines difficulty and consequences

---

### 2. MECHANICAL ADVANTAGE
**What it grants**: Better chance to succeed at specific things

**Examples**:
- Enforcer's +3 Body: Mechanically better at physical tasks
- Hacker's +4 Code: Best at digital intrusion
- Face's +3 Cool: Superior social manipulation

**Player authority**: Establishes competency in domain  
**GM authority**: Adjudicates rolls normally

---

### 3. NARRATIVE AUTHORITY
**What it grants**: Permission to declare truth about the fiction

**Examples**:
- Infiltrator's Backdoor Specialist: "I automatically get in—I left myself access"
- Nomad's Know the Routes: "I declare a route exists"
- Face's Perfect Lie: "I fabricate complete identity with documentation"
- Hacker's Ghost in the Machine: "I'm completely untraceable for this action"

**Player authority**: Declares what's true, introduces elements  
**GM authority**: Introduces complications, never blocks

The third category is what makes this game different. Players don't just roll to see if they succeed—they declare fiction and the GM responds with complications.

## HOW ASPECTS CREATE NARRATIVE LEVERS

### Aspects Are Always True
Your High Concept, Trouble, and Connection aren't just flavor text. They're **facts about the world**.

If your High Concept is "Ex-Corporate Security Auditor," then:
- You actually worked corporate security (true in fiction)
- You have that knowledge and those contacts (true in fiction)
- You left under circumstances (true in fiction)
- Other characters know this about you (true in fiction)

**This means**: Aspects create fictional positioning automatically.

---

### Aspects Can Be Invoked
Spend a Fate point when an aspect helps:
- +2 to roll after rolling, OR
- Reroll all dice

You must explain how aspect helps in this specific situation.

**This means**: You can pull mechanical levers when your character identity matters.

**Example**:
"My High Concept is *Golgotha Mine Enforcer Who Broke Her Contract*. I'm trying to intimidate this corporate security team. I invoke it—they know I used to be one of them, and I'm more dangerous for having left. +2 to Cool."

---

### Aspects Can Be Compelled
GM offers Fate point when aspect complicates situation.

**Example**:
"Your Connection is *My Sister Still Works Golgotha Security*. She's on the team hunting you right now. Take a Fate point if you can't bring yourself to hurt her?"

**This means**: Your character identity creates complications you accept for mechanical benefit (Fate points you can spend later).

---

### The Economy
- Accept compels → Gain Fate points
- Spend Fate points → Invoke aspects
- Aspects represent both character identity AND mechanical currency

**Design Function**: Players are rewarded for accepting complications that make stories more interesting.

---

## HOW CHROME CREATES IDENTITY

### Chrome Isn't Just Equipment
Every piece of chrome:
1. Grants mechanical benefit (better at something)
2. Shifts stress tracks (gain capacity in one area, lose in another)
3. Costs monthly maintenance (ongoing burden)
4. Has failure state (breaks in specific ways)

**This means**: Chrome is a **tradeoff**, not pure upgrade.

---

### Example: Hacker's Neural Interface Suite
- **Benefit**: +2 Code when jacked in directly
- **Cost**: +3 Systems, -2 Meat (more chrome capacity, less physical resilience)
- **Maintenance**: 800 credits/month
- **Failure**: Can't jack in, Code at -3, withdrawal symptoms

**What This Creates**:
- Character becomes more digitally powerful but physically fragile
- Ongoing financial pressure (maintenance costs)
- Vulnerability (if chrome breaks, you're crippled)
- Identity tied to augmentation (you ARE your chrome)

---

### The "Veterinarian" Detail
Most medical professionals won't touch non-chromed patients—baseline humans are referred to veterinary clinics.

**What This Reveals**:
- Chrome isn't optional in this society, it's expected
- Being purely organic marks you as lower-class
- The dystopia is casual, accepted, joked about
- Your chrome choices are statements about your relationship to power

---

### Design Function
Chrome forces players to:
1. **Specialize** (you can't chrome everything)
2. **Accept vulnerability** (breaking chrome breaks you)
3. **Engage with economy** (maintenance costs drive you to take jobs)
4. **Define identity** (what you chromed reveals who you are)

---

## HOW STRESS & CONSEQUENCES CREATE STAKES

### Why Three Stress Tracks?
- **Meat**: Physical damage (bullets, falls, toxins)
- **Nerves**: Psychological strain (fear, betrayal, pressure)
- **Systems**: Chrome and equipment damage

**Design Function**: Characters break in different ways. The enforcer tanks physical damage but cracks psychologically. The hacker's chrome fails while their body atrophies. The face manipulates until they forget who they are.

---

### Why Consequences Are Aspects
When you take consequence, you name it:
- *Cracked Ribs* (Meat consequence)
- *PTSD Flashback* (Nerves consequence)
- *Chrome Overheating* (Systems consequence)

**These consequences are aspects**, which means:
- They're true in the fiction (you have cracked ribs)
- They can be invoked (push through pain for +2)
- They can be compelled (GM: "Your ribs make running difficult. Take Fate point?")
- They define your current state

**Design Function**: Failure isn't binary. You accumulate damage that becomes part of your character, creates complications, but also gives you mechanical options (invoke your consequence when it helps).

---

### The Failure Spiral (And How To Escape It)
As you take consequences, you become more vulnerable:
- You have fewer stress boxes available
- Your consequences can be compelled against you
- Your effectiveness decreases

**But**:
- You also have more aspects to invoke (consequences are aspects)
- You gain Fate points from compels
- You can concede before being taken out

**Design Function**: Losing slowly is interesting. The game rewards meaningful retreat (concession) over fighting to death.

---

## HOW FATE POINTS CREATE NARRATIVE ECONOMY

### The Fate Point Cycle
1. GM compels your aspect → You gain Fate point
2. You spend Fate point → Invoke aspect for +2 or reroll
3. You accept more compels → Gain more Fate points
4. Repeat

**What This Creates**:
- Players are rewarded for accepting complications
- Accepting complications now gives power later
- The story becomes more interesting (complications create drama)
- Players have agency over when to spend their accumulated power

---

### Example In Play
**Turn 1**: GM compels your Trouble *"I Follow Orders, Even Bad Ones"* - your employer orders you to do something questionable. You accept, gain Fate point.

**Turn 3**: You're trying to intimidate corporate security. You invoke your High Concept *"Former Sidereal Security"* for +2, spend the Fate point you gained earlier.

**Turn 5**: GM compels your Connection *"My Sister Still Works Station Security"* - she's part of the response team. You accept, gain Fate point.

**Turn 7**: You're wounded, need to make a desperate escape. You invoke your consequence *"Bullet Wound"* (pushing through pain) for +2, spend the Fate point.

**What Just Happened**: 
- You accepted two complications that made the story more dramatic
- You spent that narrative currency to succeed at critical moments
- Your character's identity (aspects) directly influenced mechanical outcomes
- The story became more interesting because complications snowballed

---

### Design Function
Fate points are **narrative currency**. They represent:
- Your willingness to accept complications
- Your ability to lean into character identity
- Your control over when your character shines

This creates a rhythm: Accept complications → Gain power → Spend power → Accept more complications.

## HOW PLAYBOOKS DISTRIBUTE AUTHORITY

### Each Playbook Grants Different Narrative Permissions

| Playbook | Primary Authority | What You Can Declare |
|----------|------------------|---------------------|
| **Infiltrator** | Access & Information | "I've been here before," "I know someone," "I left a backdoor" |
| **Influencer** | Attention & Audience | "My followers are watching," "My sponsors arranged this" |

### What This Means In Play

**Without Playbook Authority**:
- Player: "Can I say I've been to this facility before?"
- GM: "Hmm, maybe? Let's see..."
- (Uncertainty about who has authority)

**With Playbook Authority**:
- Player (Infiltrator): "I use Backdoor Specialist. I audited this facility before—I'm automatically in."
- GM: "Okay, you're in. You find the security logs have been wiped recently. Someone else was here."

---

### Design Function
Each playbook gives players **explicit permission** to introduce specific types of fiction. The GM doesn't gatekeep—they introduce complications to player declarations.

This creates **collaborative storytelling** where:
- Players declare truths about the world (within their domain)
- GM introduces complications and consequences
- Story emerges from negotiation, not from one person controlling everything

## TEACHING THE GAME

### Session Zero: Three Steps

**Step 1: Choose Playbooks**
Each player reads their playbook and explains to the group:
- "My character can declare..."
- "I have narrative authority over..."
- "My signature move lets me..."

**Step 2: Establish Relationships**
Use Phase Four from playbooks:
- Who do you know?
- What complication exists?

This creates crew connections before play starts.

**Step 3: Explain Moves Sheet**
Walk through:
- When you roll vs when you don't
- How aspects work (invoke/compel)
- Fate point economy
- Stress and consequences

**First Session**:
- Start with action (heist, chase, infiltration)
- Call for rolls when stakes matter
- Offer compels early ("Your Trouble complicates this...")
- Show how invokes work ("Spend a Fate point for +2")
- Let players use their signature moves

---

### What Players Learn Through Play

**First Session**: Basic rolls, when to use stats, how stress works

**Second Session**: Invoking aspects, accepting compels, Fate point economy

**Third Session**: Creating advantages, using consequences as aspects, playbook moves

**By Fourth Session**: System is internalized, players drive narration, GM introduces complications

---

### The Learning Curve

**Easy to start**: Pick playbook, roll dice when GM calls for it

**Medium complexity**: Understand when to invoke/accept compels, manage Fate points

**System mastery**: Know when to create advantages, leverage consequences, use playbook authority to maximum effect

**Design Function**: Game is accessible to new players but rewards system mastery. You can play effectively from session one, but improve as you learn the levers.

## THE DESIGN PHILOSOPHY

### Make The Implicit Explicit
Every mechanic does work. No mechanic is just flavor.

- Aspects → Both fiction and mechanics
- Chrome → Both identity and tradeoff
- Playbooks → Both archetype and authority grant
- Fate points → Both reward and currency
- Consequences → Both damage and new aspects

**Nothing is "just roleplaying."** Everything that matters has mechanical support.

---

### Show Them The Levers
Players should know what levers they can pull:

"I can spend a Fate point here for +2"  
"I can invoke my High Concept because it's relevant"  
"I can use my Backdoor Specialist talent—no roll needed"  
"I can accept this compel and gain a Fate point"

**Design Function**: Transparency about mechanical options enables informed player choices.

---

### Distribute Authority Fairly But Unevenly
Not everyone can declare everything, but everyone can declare something:

- Infiltrator declares access and backdoors
- Hacker declares digital omniscience
- Face declares social manipulation
- Nomad declares routes and mobility
- Enforcer declares violence and protection
- Influencer declares attention and audience

**No playbook is more powerful**—they have authority over different domains.

**Design Function**: Niche protection + clear roles = everyone contributes uniquely.

---

### Make Failure Interesting
Never roll when failure is boring.

When you roll and fail:
- You take stress (accumulating damage)
- You take consequences (new aspects)
- Situation complicates
- New opportunities emerge

**Design Function**: Failure doesn't stop story—it redirects it.

---

### Reward Accepting Complications
The Fate point economy means:
- Accepting compels → Gain currency
- Spending currency → Invoke aspects
- More complications → Better story + more power

**Design Function**: Players are mechanically rewarded for making their lives harder, which creates better stories.
